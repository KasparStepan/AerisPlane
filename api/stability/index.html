    Stability API¶

    Module: aerisplane.stability

    Quick start¶

    import aerisplane as ap
from aerisplane import weights, stability

# Build aircraft
aircraft = ap.Aircraft(name="my_uav", wings=[main_wing, htail, vtail],
                       fuselages=[fuse])

# Weight analysis first — CG is required for all stability work
weight_result = weights.analyze(aircraft)

# Basic static stability at a cruise condition
condition = ap.FlightCondition(velocity=18.0, altitude=100.0, alpha=4.0)
result = stability.analyze(aircraft, condition, weight_result)
print(result.report())
result.plot()

    Theory¶

    Axes and sign conventions¶

    AerisPlane uses the stability-axes convention (standard for fixed-wing analysis): - x forward, y right, z down - Positive roll (p) right wing down - Positive pitch (q) nose up - Positive yaw (r) nose right

    All aerodynamic moment coefficients are normalised by q·S·c (pitching, Cm) or q·S·b (rolling Cl, yawing Cn), where S is the reference wing area, c is the MAC, and b is the span.

    Longitudinal static stability¶

    The neutral point and static margin¶

    Static stability in pitch is characterised by the slope of the pitching moment coefficient with angle of attack, measured about the centre of gravity:

    dCm/dα < 0  →  statically stable

    If alpha increases (e.g. a gust), a stable aircraft produces a nose-down moment that restores the original trim condition.

    The neutral point (NP) is the special CG position where dCm/dα = 0 — the aircraft has neutral stability. It can be found from the relationship between the pitching moment and lift slopes:

    x_NP = x_CG − (dCm/dα) / (dCL/dα) · c̄

    This is the formula implemented in compute_derivatives(). Note that dCm/dα and dCL/dα are both taken about the same moment reference (the CG), which is why the aircraft copy has xyz_ref set to the CG before every aero evaluation.

    The static margin (SM) is the normalised distance between the NP and the CG:

    SM = (x_NP − x_CG) / c̄

    Positive SM means the NP is aft of the CG — the aircraft is statically stable. A 10 % MAC static margin means the NP is 10 % of the wing chord behind the CG.

    Cm-alpha curve¶

    The full Cm(α) relationship determines whether the aircraft can be trimmed (Cm = 0) at a flyable angle of attack. A well-designed aircraft has: - Negative slope (dCm/dα < 0) — stable - Positive Cm at zero lift — ensures trim occurs at a positive alpha (lifting condition)

    The trim alpha is the root of Cm(α) = 0. AerisPlane finds it with Brent's method over a ±10° bracket around the linear estimate.

    Lift curve slope¶

    The lift curve slope dCL/dα (CL_alpha) is mainly a function of aspect ratio and sweep. For an elliptical lift distribution it approaches the Prandtl limit 2π/rad ≈ 0.110/deg. For real tapered wings of AR 5–10, expect 0.07–0.10/deg from VLM.

    Lateral-directional static stability¶

    Dihedral effect (Cl_beta)¶

    When the aircraft sideslips (β > 0, nose left relative to flow), a negative roll moment is generated by geometric dihedral, wing sweep, and high-wing placement. This rolling restores wings-level:

    dCl/dβ < 0  →  stable dihedral effect

    Physical sources: geometric dihedral (dominant for low-wing aircraft), wing sweep (swept-back wings have a strong dihedral effect), and vertical position of the wing relative to the CG (high wing adds stability, low wing reduces it).

    Too much dihedral effect combined with insufficient yaw damping can cause Dutch roll — a coupled oscillation between roll and yaw that is uncomfortable or hard to control. For RC aircraft, moderate Cl_beta (−0.01 to −0.05 /deg) is desirable.

    Weathercock stability (Cn_beta)¶

    When the aircraft sideslips, a positive yawing moment yaws the nose back into the wind:

    dCn/dβ > 0  →  directionally stable (weathercock effect)

    The vertical tail is the primary contributor. Its moment arm and area set the directional stability level. Insufficient Cn_beta results in spiral divergence or directional instability.

    Tail volume coefficients¶

    Tail volume coefficients are dimensionless sizing parameters that capture how much tail authority an aircraft has, independently of flight speed or wing loading.

    Horizontal tail volume: 

    Vh = (S_ht · l_ht) / (S_w · c̄_w)

    where S_ht is the horizontal tail area, l_ht is the distance from the wing aerodynamic centre to the tail aerodynamic centre, S_w is the wing area, and c̄_w is the wing MAC.

    Vh governs longitudinal trim authority and pitch stability contribution of the tail. A larger Vh moves the neutral point further aft relative to the CG, increasing static margin.

    Vertical tail volume: 

    Vv = (S_vt · l_vt) / (S_w · b_w)

    where b_w is the wing span. Vv governs directional stability and rudder authority.

    Typical values for the RC/UAV class (1–20 kg):

    Parameter Typical range Notes
    Vh 0.35–0.60 Hand-launched UAVs trend higher (0.45–0.60)
    Vv 0.03–0.06 Flying wings use Vv from twin boom fins

    Values below the lower bound usually indicate insufficient tail authority — the aircraft will be hard to trim or directionally weak. Values above the upper bound add unnecessary drag and weight.

    Finite-difference derivatives¶

    All stability derivatives are computed by central finite differences. For a generic derivative dX/dα:

    dX/dα ≈ [X(α + Δα) − X(α − Δα)] / (2·Δα)

    Central differences are second-order accurate in Δα (error ∝ Δα²), versus first-order for forward differences (error ∝ Δα). For a linear solver like VLM this makes no difference — the derivative is exact regardless of step size — but for viscous or nonlinear solvers the central formula removes the leading error term and gives a better result at a given step size.

    Step size selection:

    A step too large violates the linearity assumption (the derivative is evaluated over a nonlinear part of the polar). A step too small amplifies numerical noise from the aero solver. For VLM (inviscid, linear), the step only needs to be large enough to avoid floating-point cancellation:

    Variable Step Typical ΔX per step (VLM)
    α ±0.5 deg ΔCL ≈ 0.04, ΔCm ≈ 0.002
    β ±1.0 deg ΔCl ≈ 0.005, ΔCn ≈ 0.005
    q̂, p̂, r̂ ±0.001 ΔCm ≈ 0.001–0.005

    For viscous or nonlinear solvers (NLL, AeroBuildup), the α and β steps are small enough to stay within the linear regime below stall.

    Rate derivatives and dynamic stability¶

    Nondimensional rates¶

    Body-axis angular rates are nondimensionalised against the reference dimensions and airspeed:

    p̂ = p·b / (2V)    (roll rate hat)
q̂ = q·c̄ / (2V)   (pitch rate hat)
r̂ = r·b / (2V)    (yaw rate hat)

    These nondimensional rates are the natural arguments for aerodynamic moment derivatives because they represent the ratio of rotational tip speed to flight speed — the aerodynamically relevant quantity.

    Short-period mode¶

    The short-period mode is a fast oscillation in angle of attack and pitch rate at roughly constant airspeed. Its frequency and damping depend on the pitch stiffness (Cm_alpha) and pitch damping (Cm_q):

    ω_sp² = (q·S·c̄ / I_yy) · [CL_α·|Cm_q| − |Cm_α|·(CL_α + CL_q)]

ζ_sp  = (−Cm_q) · (q·S·c̄²) / (2·I_yy·V·ω_sp)

    where I_yy is the pitch moment of inertia about the CG and all angle derivatives are in 1/rad (CL_α = CL_alpha × 180/π).

    The formula requires both Cm_alpha < 0 (pitch stable) and Cm_q < 0 (pitch damping present) for a positive discriminant. If either condition is violated, the short-period frequency is returned as NaN.

    Design targets for the short-period:

    Parameter Minimum Typical target Notes
    ω_sp > 0 2–10 rad/s Too slow → sluggish in pitch
    ζ_sp > 0.35 0.5–1.0 Below 0.35 → noticeable oscillation after disturbance

    Phugoid mode¶

    The phugoid is a slow exchange between kinetic and potential energy at roughly constant angle of attack. The Lanchester approximation gives:

    ω_ph = g·√2 / V

ζ_ph = (CD / CL) / √2

    The phugoid frequency depends only on speed — slower aircraft phugoid more slowly. Damping depends on the drag-to-lift ratio: cleaner aircraft (lower CD/CL) have less phugoid damping and oscillate longer.

    For RC/UAV class aircraft flying at 10–20 m/s, the phugoid period is typically 5–15 s. Phugoid damping of 0.01–0.05 is normal — the mode is usually lightly damped but stable and slow enough to correct manually.

    Yaw-roll coupling¶

    Cn_p (adverse yaw) and Cl_r (roll due to yaw) are coupling derivatives that appear in the Dutch roll mode. For a basic analysis:

    • Adverse yaw (Cn_p < 0): differential drag when rolling causes a yawing moment opposing the turn. Differential aileron deflection worsens it; Frise ailerons or rudder mixing reduces it.
    • Proverse yaw (Cn_p > 0): swept wings can produce proverse yaw (unusual on RC aircraft).
    • Roll due to yaw (Cl_r > 0): when the aircraft yaws right, the left wing (advancing) produces more lift, rolling the aircraft right into the turn — a destabilising coupling in Dutch roll.

    Trim¶

    Trim is the condition where the net aerodynamic moment about the CG is zero and lift equals weight in steady level flight. Two equations must be satisfied simultaneously:

    Cm(α_trim, δ_e_trim) = 0        (moment balance)
CL(α_trim, δ_e_trim) = W / (q·S)  (lift equals weight)

    For a conventional tailed aircraft this is solved sequentially: 1. Find α_trim where Cm = 0 with δ_e = 0 (or at the baseline deflection). 2. Find δ_e_trim where Cm = 0 at α_trim while also satisfying the lift condition.

    AerisPlane implements step 1 as a Brent root-find over a ±10° alpha bracket, then step 2 as a Brent root-find over ±25° elevator deflection. If either bracket does not contain a sign change, a warning is raised and the linear estimate (step 1) or NaN (step 2) is returned.

    Trim alpha depends on: CG position, wing camber, tail incidence, and airspeed (through the required CL = W/qS — lower speed requires higher alpha).

    Trim elevator depends on: CG position (more aft CG requires more up elevator to maintain Cm = 0), tail arm, and elevator effectiveness (CL_delta_e).

    CG envelope¶

    The CG envelope defines the range of CG positions over which the aircraft is safely trimmable and controllable. AerisPlane computes it from the neutral point with a fixed 5 % MAC safety margin:

    CG_aft_limit  = x_NP − 5 % MAC        (minimum stability margin)
CG_fwd_limit  = x_NP − SM − 5 % MAC   (elevator authority limit, minimum 5 % from LE)

    The forward limit reflects the elevator's ability to hold the nose up at low speed (high alpha, high lift required). Moving the CG too far forward increases the download required from the tail, which the elevator may not be able to provide, especially near stall.

    The aft limit reflects the minimum acceptable static margin. Flying with the CG at or beyond the neutral point means any pitch disturbance grows rather than damps.

    Typical RC/UAV CG operating range: 15–35 % MAC. Performance-oriented aircraft (sailplanes, racing UAVs) fly at 25–35 % MAC for low trim drag; docile trainers at 15–25 % MAC for more stability margin.

    analyze()¶

    aerisplane.stability.analyze(
    aircraft,
    condition,
    weight_result,
    aero_method="vlm",
    compute_rate_derivatives=False,
    **aero_kwargs,
) -> StabilityResult

    Runs a full static (and optionally dynamic) stability analysis.

    Steps performed internally: 1. Compute stability derivatives via central finite differences (5 aero evaluations). 2. Find trim alpha and trim elevator using Brent's method. 3. Compute horizontal and vertical tail volume coefficients. 4. Determine CG envelope limits from the neutral point. 5. Estimate dynamic stability modes (short-period, phugoid) if rate derivatives are requested.

    Parameters¶

    Parameter Type Default Description
    aircraft Aircraft — Aircraft geometry definition
    condition FlightCondition — Operating point (speed, altitude, alpha, beta)
    weight_result WeightResult — Provides CG position and total mass
    aero_method str "vlm" Aero solver — passed to aero.analyze()
    compute_rate_derivatives bool False Also compute p, q, r rate derivatives and dynamic modes (6 extra aero calls)
    **aero_kwargs Extra keyword arguments forwarded to aero.analyze()

    Number of aero evaluations¶

    Mode Evaluations What is computed
    Default (static only) 5 CL_alpha, Cm_alpha, Cl_beta, Cn_beta; trim; NP; CG envelope
    compute_rate_derivatives=True 11 All of the above plus CL_q, Cm_q, Cl_p, Cn_p, CY_p, Cn_r, Cl_r, CY_r and dynamic modes

    StabilityResult¶

    Returned by analyze(). All quantities are in SI units unless otherwise noted.

    Longitudinal static stability¶

    Field Unit Description
    static_margin fraction of MAC NP position ahead of CG. Positive = statically stable. Typical RC target: 5–15 % MAC.
    neutral_point m Neutral point x-position from the aircraft nose
    Cm_alpha 1/deg Pitching moment derivative. Negative = statically stable.
    CL_alpha 1/deg Lift curve slope (typically 0.07–0.12 /deg for conventional wings)
    CL_baseline — Lift coefficient at the analysis condition
    Cm_baseline — Pitching moment coefficient at the analysis condition

    Lateral-directional static stability¶

    Field Unit Description
    Cl_beta 1/deg Roll due to sideslip (dihedral effect). Negative = stable — aircraft rolls into the wind when sideslipped.
    Cn_beta 1/deg Yaw due to sideslip (weathercock stability). Positive = stable — aircraft yaws into the wind when sideslipped.

    Rate derivatives¶

    These fields are None when compute_rate_derivatives=False.

    All rate derivatives are per unit nondimensional rate (e.g. q̂ = qc/2V, p̂ = pb/2V, r̂ = rb/2V).

    Field Nondim. rate Description
    CL_q qc/2V Lift due to pitch rate
    Cm_q qc/2V Pitch damping — negative = damped short-period
    Cl_p pb/2V Roll damping — negative = damped roll response
    Cn_p pb/2V Adverse yaw due to roll rate — negative = adverse yaw
    CY_p pb/2V Side force due to roll rate
    Cn_r rb/2V Yaw damping — negative = damped yaw response
    Cl_r rb/2V Roll due to yaw rate
    CY_r rb/2V Side force due to yaw rate

    Dynamic stability modes¶

    These fields are None when rate derivatives are not computed, and NaN when the computation is numerically indeterminate (e.g. Cm_alpha and Cm_q have the wrong sign combination for the short-period formula).

    Field Unit Description
    sp_frequency rad/s Short-period natural frequency
    sp_damping — Short-period damping ratio (ζ). Positive = oscillation decays.
    ph_frequency rad/s Phugoid natural frequency (Lanchester approximation: g√2/V)
    ph_damping — Phugoid damping ratio (Lanchester: CD/CL/√2). Typically low (0.01–0.05).

    Trim¶

    Field Unit Description
    trim_alpha deg Angle of attack where Cm = 0 at the current CG
    trim_elevator deg Elevator deflection for steady level flight. NaN if no elevator is found or elevator lacks authority (Cm does not cross zero in ±25°).

    Tail volume coefficients¶

    Tail volume coefficients are standard sizing parameters for preliminary design. Typical RC/UAV values: Vh ≈ 0.35–0.60, Vv ≈ 0.03–0.06.

    Field Description
    Vh Horizontal tail volume: S_ht · l_ht / (S_w · c̄_w). NaN if no horizontal tail found.
    Vv Vertical tail volume: S_vt · l_vt / (S_w · b_w). NaN if no vertical tail found.

    Tail identification uses wing geometry (symmetric → horizontal, non-symmetric → vertical) and name hints ("vtail", "vertical").

    CG envelope¶

    Field Description
    cg_forward_limit Forward CG limit as fraction of MAC from MAC leading edge
    cg_aft_limit Aft CG limit as fraction of MAC from MAC leading edge

    Both limits include a 5 % MAC safety margin from the neutral point. The aft limit is NP − 5 % MAC; the forward limit is NP − SM − 5 % MAC (minimum 5 % from LE).

    Reference values¶

    Field Unit Description
    cg_x m CG x-position used for this analysis
    mac m Mean aerodynamic chord of the main wing
    mac_le_x m MAC leading edge x-position

    Methods¶

    report()¶

    result.report() -> str

    Returns a formatted plain-text stability report. Print it with print(result.report()).

    Sections always included: longitudinal static stability, lateral-directional static stability, trim, tail volume coefficients, CG envelope, and baseline coefficients.

    Sections shown only when rate derivatives are available: rate derivatives table, dynamic stability modes (short-period and phugoid frequencies, periods, and damping ratios).

    plot()¶

    result.plot() -> matplotlib.figure.Figure

    Returns a 1×2 figure (14×6 inches):

    • Left panel — Pitch Stability: Cm vs alpha. If an alpha sweep was stored (e.g. from a polar run), the full curve is plotted. Otherwise a tangent line ±3° around the trim alpha is shown. Trim alpha is marked with a vertical dotted line.
    • Right panel — CG Envelope: A MAC bar (LE at 0, TE at 1) with the allowable CG range shaded, the current CG marked with a triangle, and the neutral point with a diamond. Forward and aft CG limits are drawn as dashed red lines.

    compute_derivatives()¶

    Lower-level function used internally by analyze(). Call this directly when you only need the raw finite-difference derivatives without trim or tail volume computation.

    from aerisplane.stability.derivatives import compute_derivatives

deriv = compute_derivatives(
    aircraft,
    condition,
    weight_result,
    aero_method="vlm",
    compute_rate_derivatives=False,
    **aero_kwargs,
) -> DerivativeResult

    Parameters¶

    Same as analyze(). The aircraft's moment reference is overridden internally to the CG from weight_result.

    Finite-difference step sizes¶

    Perturbation Step Rationale
    α (angle of attack) ±0.5 deg Small enough for linearity, large enough to avoid VLM noise
    β (sideslip) ±1.0 deg Larger step because lateral derivatives are smaller
    p̂, q̂, r̂ (nondimensional rate) ±0.001 Standard nondimensional rate perturbation

    DerivativeResult fields¶

    Field Unit Description
    CL_alpha 1/deg dCL/dα
    Cm_alpha 1/deg dCm/dα
    Cl_beta 1/deg dCl/dβ
    Cn_beta 1/deg dCn/dβ
    neutral_point m Neutral point x-position
    static_margin fraction of MAC (NP − CG) / MAC
    cg_x m CG x-position used
    mac m Reference chord
    mac_le_x m MAC leading edge x-position
    baseline AeroResult Full aero result at the analysis condition
    CL_q, Cm_q per qc/2V Pitch rate derivatives (None if not computed)
    Cl_p, Cn_p, CY_p per pb/2V Roll rate derivatives (None if not computed)
    Cn_r, Cl_r, CY_r per rb/2V Yaw rate derivatives (None if not computed)

    Sign conventions¶

    Sign Physical meaning
    Cm_alpha < 0 Statically stable — nose pitches down when alpha increases
    Cl_beta < 0 Dihedral effect — roll restores wings level when sideslipped
    Cn_beta > 0 Weathercock — yaws into the wind when sideslipped
    Cm_q < 0 Pitch rate damps short-period oscillation
    Cl_p < 0 Roll damping — roll rate decays without input
    Cn_r < 0 Yaw damping — yaw rate decays without input

    Dependencies¶

    weights.analyze() → WeightResult (CG, mass, inertia)
       ↓
stability.analyze() → StabilityResult
       ↑ uses aero.analyze() internally (5 or 11 calls)

    analyze() requires a WeightResult — always run weights.analyze() first.

    Examples¶

    Stability check at cruise¶

    import aerisplane as ap
from aerisplane import weights, stability

weight_result = weights.analyze(aircraft)
condition = ap.FlightCondition(velocity=18.0, altitude=100.0, alpha=3.0)

result = stability.analyze(aircraft, condition, weight_result)
print(result.report())

# Check stability margins
print(f"Static margin  : {result.static_margin * 100:.1f} % MAC")
print(f"Cm_alpha       : {result.Cm_alpha:+.5f} /deg")
print(f"Cn_beta (wc)   : {result.Cn_beta:+.5f} /deg")
print(f"Trim alpha     : {result.trim_alpha:.1f} deg")

    Full analysis with dynamic modes¶

    result = stability.analyze(
    aircraft, condition, weight_result,
    compute_rate_derivatives=True,
)

# Short-period period and damping
import numpy as np
if result.sp_frequency and not np.isnan(result.sp_frequency):
    sp_period = 2 * np.pi / result.sp_frequency
    print(f"Short-period: T={sp_period:.2f} s, ζ={result.sp_damping:.3f}")

# Phugoid period
if result.ph_frequency and not np.isnan(result.ph_frequency):
    ph_period = 2 * np.pi / result.ph_frequency
    print(f"Phugoid: T={ph_period:.1f} s, ζ={result.ph_damping:.4f}")

    CG sensitivity sweep¶

    Evaluate how static margin changes as CG moves fore and aft (e.g. during a payload trade).

    import numpy as np
from aerisplane.weights.result import ComponentOverride

cg_offsets = np.linspace(-0.05, 0.05, 9)  # ±50 mm from nominal
margins = []

for dx in cg_offsets:
    # Shift payload CG by dx to move total CG
    result_w = weights.analyze(aircraft, overrides={
        "payload": ComponentOverride(
            mass=0.200,
            cg=np.array([nominal_cg_x + dx, 0.0, 0.0]),
        )
    })
    result_s = stability.analyze(aircraft, condition, result_w)
    margins.append(result_s.static_margin * 100)

for dx, sm in zip(cg_offsets * 1000, margins):
    print(f"CG offset {dx:+.0f} mm → static margin {sm:.1f} % MAC")

    Sweep alpha to capture the Cm(α) curve¶

    The trim solver uses a single linear estimate. For a full Cm-vs-alpha sweep, call compute_derivatives() directly and interrogate the baseline result at each alpha.

    from aerisplane.stability.derivatives import compute_derivatives
import numpy as np

alphas = np.linspace(-4, 14, 10)
Cms = []
for a in alphas:
    cond = ap.FlightCondition(velocity=18.0, altitude=100.0, alpha=a)
    deriv = compute_derivatives(aircraft, cond, weight_result)
    Cms.append(deriv.baseline.Cm)

# Find trim alpha by interpolation
trim_a = np.interp(0.0, Cms[::-1], alphas[::-1])
print(f"Trim alpha (from sweep) = {trim_a:.2f} deg")

    report() ¶

    Formatted plain-text stability analysis report.

    Sections always included: • Longitudinal Static Stability — CL_alpha, Cm_alpha, neutral point, CG position, static margin, and a STABLE / UNSTABLE verdict. • Lateral-Directional Static Stability — Cl_beta (dihedral effect) and Cn_beta (weathercock), each with an OK / WARN flag. • Trim — trim alpha and trim elevator (or N/A if unavailable). • Tail Volume Coefficients — Vh and Vv (or N/A if not identified). • CG Envelope — forward and aft limits as % MAC, with current CG. • Baseline Coefficients — CL and Cm at the analysis condition.

    Sections shown only when rate derivatives were computed: • Rate Derivatives — CL_q, Cm_q (pitch damping), Cl_p, Cn_p (roll damping, adverse yaw), Cn_r, Cl_r (yaw damping). • Dynamic Stability Modes — short-period ω, T, ζ and phugoid ω, T, ζ (Lanchester approximation); N/A printed when computation failed.

    Returns:

    Type Description
    str

    Multi-line string suitable for printing to a terminal or saving to a text file. No trailing newline.

    plot() ¶

    Static stability summary figure (two panels).

    Left panel — Pitch Stability (Cm vs alpha): If a Cm-alpha sweep was stored in _alpha_sweep / _Cm_sweep (e.g. from a polar run), the full curve is plotted. Otherwise a tangent line spanning ±3° around trim_alpha is drawn, labelled with Cm_alpha. A dashed horizontal line marks Cm = 0, and a dotted vertical line marks the trim angle of attack.

    Right panel — CG Envelope: A horizontal bar representing the full MAC (leading edge at 0, trailing edge at 1) is drawn in light grey. The allowable CG range (forward_limit to aft_limit, both as fractions of MAC) is shaded. The current CG is marked with a downward triangle and the neutral point with a diamond. Dashed and dash-dot red lines show the forward and aft CG limits. LE / TE labels appear below the bar. The figure title states the static margin in % MAC.

    Figure
